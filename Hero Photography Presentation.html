01 · The Standard

What Makes a Good Hero Photo

Across every shot we reviewed, the same handful of factors decided "hero" versus "almost." These criteria are drawn from the notes on each photo and the Cummings brand photography guidelines, distilled into a checklist you can shoot against.

1

Eyes & Glare Control

The single most common disqualifier in our set. Eyes must read clearly through safety glasses, because glare hides the eyes and instantly weakens trust. If a reflection is unavoidable, make it intentional (let it carry the skyline or environment) rather than a shapeless hotspot.

Our most common disqualifier
2

One Clear Subject

A strong single focal point, clear of distractions. Avoid a second person actively working, competing bright areas (open doorways, windows behind the subject), or anything that splits attention.

Brand rule: one obvious hero
3

Action or Intentional Pose

Either authentically working, with hands, tool, and gaze converging on a real task, or posed safely away from activity. Never pose while operating machinery or imply that safety or focus was interrupted for the photo.

"This is how the work gets done."
4

A Story & Context

The frame should answer "what is happening here?" The background should relate to the work and add visual interest without clutter. When a shot has nothing to say, do not force it.

Context beats a pretty backdrop
5

Visible Branding

The Cummings logo should be clearly in view, on the vest or the hardhat. Headwear logo treatment works reliably, which is why branded headbands, headlamps, and hardhats are a real equipment need.

Invest in branded headwear
6

PPE & Wardrobe (When Possible)

We shoot on active job sites, so attire is not always something we can control or change. When the opportunity exists, look for fitted hi-vis that is not scrunched and attire that reads clean and professional. Treat it as a consideration to optimize when you can, not a requirement you can always stage.

Optimize when the moment allows
7

Exposure & Focus

Get correct exposure on the hero, not just the face, and keep the shot tack sharp. Light the subject properly on site whenever possible so we avoid leaning too heavily on post. Slow down for the shot; too many frames are dim, blurred, or soft.

Light it right on site
8

Intentional Composition

Compose with a marketing lens on. More than anything, be intentional about the shot. Accommodating a layout (negative space, an off-frame eye-line for copy) is a nice-to-have on some heroes, not a must, as long as the photo tells the whole story on its own.

Be intentional, tell the whole story

Two Types of Hero, and When to Use Each

From the Cummings brand guidelines, every hero photo is one of two styles. Knowing the intent keeps the pose, environment, and expression aligned.

Style A

Working Hero

Captures the team actively engaged in their craft: terminating equipment, reviewing plans, performing detailed work. The subject is focused, precise, and fully engaged. Best for active job sites and technical environments where credibility and expertise matter most.

Focal point: competency & expertise
Style B

Customer-Facing Hero

A more approachable, people-forward image. The subject may look at the camera, smile, and appear open and confident, friendly and inviting, reinforcing trust. Shot in controlled environments, never staged to look like active work, and never with others working nearby.

Focal point: trust & approachability

Do's & Don'ts at a Glance

Pulled from what actually worked and what failed across our own photos, with a couple of brand-guideline rules woven in.

✓ Do

  • ✓ Capture toward the action so the shot clearly ties the subject to what is being worked on.
  • ✓ Keep the eyes clearly visible, with no glare on the glasses.
  • ✓ Use an out-of-focus, visually interesting background that relates to the work.
  • ✓ Let the subject pose safely away from active work, keeping a confident, in-work-mode appeal.
  • ✓ Give the hands a clear purpose, like holding a tool or working the equipment.
  • ✓ Make sure the Cummings logo is clearly placed on the vest or hardhat.
  • ✓ Embrace authentic detail (even slightly messy cables) when it supports the story.
Derived from: 4N1A3211, CJ0A7173, UCS Hero, Hero 4

✗ Don't

  • ✗ Let glare on the glasses hide the eyes.
  • ✗ Pose so the vest is scrunched or the PPE looks messy and unprofessional.
  • ✗ Use clasped or hidden hands that read as awkward.
  • ✗ Force a shot with no story or unclear context.
  • ✗ Let a competing bright area, like a doorway or window behind the subject, split focus.
  • ✗ Use an over-the-shoulder, caught-in-the-moment glance with no visible task.
  • ✗ Pose on or while operating machinery in a way that looks unsafe.
Derived from: 4N1A3426, 4N1A4058, AJ Spear, 4N1A3782

04 · Not Approved

Almost Heroes, the Most Valuable Lessons

Every near-miss taught us something specific. These are organized by the one thing that pushed each into the "no" box.

Not Approved Customer Hero · Commercial

Commercial Customer Hero

Why it doesn't

Glare on the glasses kills it. The framing is compelling but the background lacks interest and it is unclear what environment he is in. The vest is scrunched up in the pose; it feels lazy and unprofessional.

Considerations

When posing, avoid anything that makes the PPE or subject look unprofessional or messy.

05 · How We Move Forward

Lessons Learned & the Road Ahead

The honest reality: a low hit-rate on heroes (and even "almosts") tells us the gold is there, but we do not yet reliably know how to recreate it. The biggest gap is getting subjects posed and comfortable, plus variety in shot and composition. Here is where we focus to close that gap.

Opportunities for Growth

Five areas, in priority order, where intentional practice will move the needle most.

PRIORITY 1

Camera Settings

Slow down for the right exposure and focus. Too many frames are out of focus, blurred, or too dim. Being nimble is great, but this has to be solved first. Research suggests our issues are skill-based, not gear-based.

FOCUS

Subject Guidance

Huge room to grow at guiding a subject: making them comfortable, getting natural poses, and pushing through the awkwardness. Give people something to do with their hands and capture candid responses.

FOCUS

Shot Composition

Build a compelling shot, not just a nice-to-look-at one. Always answer "what is happening?" This needs intentional practice and a few repeatable exercises drawn from this discovery period.

EQUIP

A Real Photography Kit

Too many compromises today. We need nimble lighting (heavy backlighting cannot be left to chance), point-and-shoot options, easier lens switching, branded headbands and headlamps for logo gaps, PPE on every shoot, and staging tools.

RESEARCH

Camera Discovery

Worth exploring whether some focus issues trace to the Canon system. Sony has felt faster to focus in past experience (consider A7R for photo, A7S for video). Likely a skill issue, so settings come first, but keep it open.

Before the Next Shoot, Lessons Baked Into a Checklist

Each jobsite is different; the site, time of day, and crew can change everything. Do not walk in with a single expectation. Plan for it.

1
Have backup plans. Do not schedule a shoot around one expectation that the site, lighting, or crew might not deliver.
2
Scope before committing. Do a simple job-walk or location-scout equivalent before bringing heavy photo resources. Optional, but valuable.
3
Build a shot list. Gather it ahead of time based on needs, environment, and location, before you start shooting.
4
Brief the challenges. Know the constraints up front: No logos? Messy workspace? What types of shots can we actually get, and where do we focus?
5
Kill glare on site. It is our #1 disqualifier, so check the eyes through the glasses on the spot, reposition, or make the reflection intentional.
6
Light the Hero. Carry fill or a reflector for backlit situations so the subject never falls into shadow against a blown-out background.

The Case for an Outside Photographer

Worth seriously considering, not as an admission of failure, but as the fastest way to close real skill gaps and level up the internal team.

Through this discovery period our hit-rate on heroes, and even on "almosts," has been low. The gold is clearly there, but we are not yet confident we can recreate it on demand. Our instincts lean toward an event and architectural style, while hero photography lives or dies on portrait, people-focused work. A professional who specializes in people and portraits would help us understand how to create natural poses, make a subject comfortable, capture the right shot, and know which angles work. This is where the largest gap sits.

Why consider it now

  • Low hit-rate tells us the skill, not the subject matter, is the bottleneck.
  • Getting subjects posed and comfortable is our clearest weak spot.
  • Variety in shot and composition is thin; we tend to repeat a setup.
  • We often find the gold by accident and cannot reliably reproduce it.

Skill gaps a pro closes

  • Posing and subject comfort: directing real people, not models.
  • Composition and angle variety, with intent behind every frame.
  • On-site lighting: handling heavy backlight and exposing the hero in the moment.
  • Speed and consistency: recreating the gold shot on command.

How we would work with them

  • Hand them our notes, the criteria, and the almost-heroes so the target is clear.
  • Treat it as a learning engagement, not just a deliverable.
  • Watch how they pose, light, and direct, then bring those techniques in-house.
  • Use a few guided shoots to raise our hit-rate and upskill the internal team.

A Simple Way Forward

Sequenced so each step builds on the last.

1

Fix the fundamentals

Drill camera settings: exposure, focus, and glare management, until clean, sharp frames are the default, not the exception.

2

Equip for the field

Assemble the nimble kit: portable lighting, branded headwear, point-and-shoot backup, staging tools, and PPE on every shoot.

3

Learn from a pro

Bring in a portrait and people photographer with our notes in hand, focused on posing, comfort, angle variety, and recreating the gold shot.

4

Plan every shoot

Scope, build a shot list, and brief the constraints before arriving, prepped for both a Working and a Customer hero.

5

Shoot, review, refine

Keep scoring shots against the criteria, keep the "almosts" as teaching cases, and keep tightening the standard as we go.
